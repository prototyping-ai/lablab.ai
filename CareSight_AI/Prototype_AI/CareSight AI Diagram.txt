CareSight AI Diagram:

AI-Enabled Clinical Assistant Platform
Architecture, Data Flow, and System Integration Overview

The AI-Enabled Clinical Assistant Platform is a hybrid edge-to-cloud healthcare system designed to augment clinical workflows through intelligent automation, computer vision, machine learning, and large language models (LLMs). The system supports four primary capabilities: intelligent appointment check-in, AI-powered clinical transcription, skin analysis classification, and stroke risk assessment. It is deployable via a smart kiosk or an autonomous robotic assistant within a clinical environment and integrates directly with clinical staff workflows and electronic health record (EHR) systems.

At the edge layer, the system operates on either a kiosk interface or a robotic platform equipped with a camera, microphone, speaker, and touchscreen display. This interface serves as the patient interaction point. The camera captures images for document verification, prescription updates, and skin analysis, while the microphone enables voice-based check-in, health questionnaires, and conversational engagement. MediaPipe is used locally for facial and mouth movement detection to support transcription accuracy and speaker validation. Basic preprocessing and validation occur at the edge before secure transmission to cloud-based services.

All requests from the kiosk or robotic assistant are routed through a centralized API Gateway and orchestration layer. This layer manages authentication, session control, logging, guardrail validation, and routing to the appropriate AI or machine learning service. It also contains a model-switching mechanism that allows the frontend to dynamically select between multiple LLM providers, such as ChatGPT-4o or Gemma 3N, without requiring changes to backend services. This abstraction ensures flexibility, scalability, and vendor independence.

The AI Services Layer consists of modular microservices designed for specific functions. The OCR service processes images of driver licenses, insurance cards (front and back), and prescription documents. Extracted raw text is passed to an LLM that structures the information into standardized JSON schemas compatible with clinical systems. Guardrails validate that uploaded images match expected document types before processing. A transcription service handles audio streams, applies speaker diarization to distinguish multiple participants, and generates structured clinical summaries using LLM-based summarization. Machine learning inference endpoints host CNN-based .pt models responsible for skin type classification, benign versus malignant lesion detection, and stroke risk analysis. Each ML endpoint operates independently and returns a classification label, confidence score, and structured output.

The data layer stores structured JSON outputs, risk scores, transcription summaries, and validation results in secure, encrypted storage. This layer is designed for HIPAA-compliant handling of protected health information (PHI). Role-based access control ensures that patients, nurses, doctors, and administrators only access data appropriate to their roles. Audit logging tracks all model calls, document uploads, and record modifications. The system supports integration with external EHR platforms through standardized APIs, enabling structured medical documentation, intake summaries, and risk assessments to be reviewed and optionally committed to official patient records.

The intelligent appointment check-in workflow begins when a patient interacts with the kiosk or robotic assistant. Voice-based authentication captures patient responses, which are transcribed and validated by an LLM. Driver license and insurance card images are captured and processed through OCR and structured by the LLM into machine-readable JSON. Prescription updates follow the same pipeline. The validated and structured data is presented to front desk staff and optionally synchronized with the clinic’s EHR system, significantly reducing manual intake workload.

During clinical consultations, the AI transcriber system captures multi-speaker conversations between patient and provider. MediaPipe enhances visual confirmation of speaking activity, while speech-to-text and diarization distinguish speakers. An LLM generates both conversational summaries and structured SOAP-style medical notes. These notes are made available for provider review before final approval and EHR submission. The system augments documentation efficiency without replacing clinical judgment.

For dermatological assessments, the skin analysis capability uses camera-captured images that are validated through guardrails to ensure appropriate content. Images are sent to CNN-based classification endpoints that determine skin type and malignancy risk. Results are returned with confidence scores and are explained to the patient through an LLM-generated summary. This provides both clinical decision support and patient education.

The stroke risk assessment capability is initiated through a guided voice-based questionnaire consisting of ten bio-related questions. The LLM validates completeness of responses and structures the answers into JSON format. The structured data is sent to a stroke risk ML model endpoint, which calculates a risk score. The LLM then provides a conversational explanation of the result and may recommend follow-up discussion with a clinician. This workflow ensures both analytical rigor and patient-friendly communication.

From a deployment perspective, the platform operates as a hybrid architecture. Edge devices handle image capture, audio input, and initial validation, while computationally intensive AI processing occurs in the cloud. Services are containerized to enable horizontal scaling, high availability, and load balancing. The microservices architecture ensures that each AI capability can evolve independently without impacting other components.

Security and compliance considerations are foundational to the architecture. All data is encrypted in transit using TLS and encrypted at rest within secure storage environments. Access controls enforce strict separation between patient-facing interfaces and clinician dashboards. Guardrails validate image content and model outputs to reduce misuse or incorrect data ingestion. Comprehensive logging supports audit requirements and regulatory compliance.

Overall, the architecture represents an AI-augmented clinical workflow system designed to enhance efficiency, improve documentation accuracy, support risk assessment, and streamline patient intake. It does not replace medical professionals but instead serves as an intelligent assistant embedded within the clinic environment. The modular design allows for future expansion, including additional diagnostic models, expanded EHR integrations, and enhanced robotic perception capabilities.
